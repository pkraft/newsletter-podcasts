Chip stocks post worst week since March 2025 on ‘DeepSeek moment’ fears
The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index fell about 11% for the week and roughly 24% off its late-June high, its steepest weekly drop since March 2025, as investors rotated out of AI infrastructure winners. NVIDIA dropped 3.4%, AMD fell 4.9%, and Applied Materials slid 6.5%, with the sell-off spreading to Seoul and European markets. The trigger: Moonshot AI's Kimi K3, a huge open-weight model released cheaply, revived worries — echoing January 2025's DeepSeek shock — that frontier labs' massive capital spending may not be defensible if capable models can be built for far less.
Why it matters: This is the market putting a real price on the ROI question hanging over every hyperscaler's AI capex plan, and a sustained rotation would tighten the financing environment that OpenAI, Anthropic, and NVIDIA's whole supply chain are counting on.
Apple reclaims world's-most-valuable-company title from NVIDIA
Apple closed Friday with a $4.88 trillion market cap versus NVIDIA's roughly $4.86 trillion, ending NVIDIA's nearly year-long run at the top as the chip sell-off knocked NVIDIA shares down. Apple is up 22% this year on confidence in its services and hardware-upgrade cycle rather than speculative AI infrastructure exposure; it last held the title in April 2025.
Why it matters: It's a visible marker of the same rotation driving the chip sell-off — investors rewarding AI monetization and light capex over infrastructure bets as the payoff timeline for massive AI spending gets questioned.
US clears a trickle of NVIDIA H200 shipments to China, reigniting export-control fight
A small number of NVIDIA H200 AI chips have shipped to Chinese buyers after winning US approval, with roughly 10 Chinese firms — including Alibaba, Tencent, and ByteDance — cleared to purchase up to 75,000 H200s each under license. Commerce official Jeffrey Kessler called the volume shipped so far “trivial,” and some approved Chinese buyers are reportedly holding off on finalizing purchases anyway.
Why it matters: Even token approvals reopen the political fight over whether export controls are being used as a bargaining chip with Beijing, and Chinese buyers' hesitation suggests the controls' deterrent effect may outlast the paperwork easing.

Google DeepMind safety researcher resigns over unrestricted Pentagon AI deal
Alex Turner, a Google DeepMind AI safety researcher, revealed in a July 15 blog post that he resigned in June after Google signed a Pentagon agreement for classified AI work that he says lacked restrictions on autonomous weapons and mass surveillance. Turner had proposed a military-AI safety framework requiring human control over targeting systems and sent it to DeepMind CEO Demis Hassabis, but says the deal went through without the safeguards. His departure follows roughly 600 Google employees petitioning in April against the company taking on classified military work.
Why it matters: It's a rare public resignation-on-principle from inside a frontier lab, and it puts a face on the internal tension between AI labs chasing defense contracts and the safety researchers who joined those labs on different promises.
OpenAI's head of safety systems to depart as safety folds into research
Johannes Heidecke, OpenAI's head of safety systems since Lilian Weng left to co-found Thinking Machines, is set to leave by July 24 as part of a reorganization that folds the safety function into the research org under VP of research and alignment lead Mia Glaese. Heidecke informed staff of the move in early July.
Why it matters: It's the latest in a string of safety-leadership exits at OpenAI, and folding safety under a research lead rather than keeping it as a standalone reporting line will invite fresh scrutiny of how much independent authority the function retains.

Microsoft preps ‘Project Perception,’ a cheaper multi-model rival to Anthropic's Mythos
Microsoft is developing Project Perception, an AI vulnerability-hunting tool that routes queries across Anthropic, OpenAI, and Microsoft's own models to scan enterprise systems, identify weaknesses, and propose fixes. It's positioned as a direct, lower-cost challenger to Anthropic's Mythos security agent — whose API is reportedly far pricier than Opus or GPT — and is led by Microsoft security chief Hayete Gallot, who has been reorganizing the security division around AI-driven threat detection since February.
Why it matters: It shows enterprise security becoming a proving ground for multi-model orchestration, and it puts real pricing pressure on Anthropic's Mythos just as AI-driven vulnerability discovery starts becoming a standard part of the security stack.
1Password lets Claude log into sites without ever seeing your passwords
1Password launched 1Password for Claude, built on a ‘zero-exposure’ architecture: when Claude's browser agent needs to sign in somewhere, 1Password shows the user which credential is requested and why, and on approval injects it directly into the page — the password and one-time codes never pass through the model or Anthropic's systems. A companion ‘Agentic Mode’ locks down the extension to only the approved credentials for the current task, keeping the rest of the vault out of reach. It's live now for Mac users on 1Password's business, family, and individual plans.
Why it matters: Credential handling has been one of the biggest trust blockers for letting AI agents act autonomously in the browser, and a workable zero-exposure pattern here is the kind of plumbing that determines how fast agentic browsing actually gets adopted.
